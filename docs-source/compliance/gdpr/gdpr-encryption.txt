GDPR ENCRYPTION REQUIREMENTS
================================================================================
 
OVERVIEW
--------
The GDPR does not mandate a specific encryption algorithm or strength but identifies
encryption as an explicitly recommended technical measure in multiple articles. It
also treats the presence of encryption as a mitigating factor in personal data breach
notification obligations. The regulation's risk-based approach means that encryption
is practically required for any personal data exposed to meaningful risk.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 4 - DEFINITIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 4(5) - Pseudonymisation:
  'pseudonymisation' means the processing of personal data in such a manner that the
  personal data can no longer be attributed to a specific data subject without the use
  of additional information, provided that such additional information is kept separately
  and is subject to technical and organisational measures to ensure that the personal
  data are not attributed to an identified or identifiable natural person.
 
Note: Pseudonymisation is distinct from encryption but is frequently implemented via
encryption. The GDPR treats pseudonymised data as still being personal data unless the
re-identification key is permanently destroyed.
 
Recital 26:
  Personal data which have undergone pseudonymisation, which could be attributed to a
  natural person by the use of additional information, should be considered to be
  information on an identifiable natural person.
 
Recital 28:
  The application of pseudonymisation to personal data can reduce the risks to the data
  subjects concerned and help controllers and processors to meet their data-protection
  obligations. The explicit introduction of 'pseudonymisation' in this Regulation is
  not intended to preclude any other measures of data protection.
 
Recital 29:
  In order to create incentives to apply pseudonymisation when processing personal data,
  measures of pseudonymisation should, whilst allowing general analysis, be possible
  within the same controller when that controller has taken technical and organisational
  measures necessary to ensure, for the processing concerned, that this Regulation is
  implemented, and that additional information for attributing the personal data to a
  specific data subject is kept separately.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - Where personal data is stored in cloud databases or object storage, pseudonymisation
    (e.g., tokenisation, encryption with key separation) should be applied where feasible.
  - Encryption keys must be stored separately from the encrypted data, ideally in a
    dedicated key management service (AWS KMS, Azure Key Vault, GCP Cloud KMS).
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 25 - DATA PROTECTION BY DESIGN (ENCRYPTION AS DEFAULT)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 25(1):
  The controller shall implement appropriate technical and organisational measures, such
  as pseudonymisation, which are designed to implement data-protection principles, such
  as data minimisation, in an effective manner.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - Encryption at rest must be enabled by default on all storage resources handling
    personal data (S3 buckets, RDS instances, Blob Storage accounts, Cloud SQL instances).
  - Default encryption must use strong algorithms (AES-256 or equivalent).
  - Encryption in transit must be enforced; plaintext connections must be disabled.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 32 - SECURITY OF PROCESSING (PRIMARY ENCRYPTION ARTICLE)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 32(1)(a):
  The controller and the processor shall implement appropriate technical and
  organisational measures to ensure a level of security appropriate to the risk,
  including as appropriate:
  (a) the pseudonymisation and encryption of personal data;
 
This is the primary statutory basis for encryption under the GDPR. The phrase
"as appropriate" links directly to the risk assessment under Article 32(2).
 
Article 32(1)(b):
  the ability to ensure the ongoing confidentiality, integrity, availability and
  resilience of processing systems and services;
 
Article 32(2):
  In assessing the appropriate level of security, account shall be taken in particular
  of the risks that are presented by processing, in particular from accidental or
  unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorised disclosure of, or access to,
  personal data transmitted, stored or otherwise processed.
 
Recital 83:
  In order to maintain security and to prevent processing in infringement of this
  Regulation, the controller or processor should evaluate the risks inherent in the
  processing and implement measures to mitigate those risks, such as encryption. Those
  measures should ensure an appropriate level of security, including confidentiality,
  taking into account the state of the art and the costs of implementation in relation
  to the risks and the nature of the personal data to be protected. In assessing data
  security risk, consideration should be given to the risks that are presented by
  personal data processing, such as accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration,
  unauthorised disclosure of, or access to, personal data transmitted, stored or
  otherwise processed which may in particular lead to physical, material or non-material
  damage.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - Encryption at rest: All cloud storage services (object storage, block storage,
    databases) that store personal data must have encryption at rest enabled.
  - Encryption in transit: All API endpoints and data transfer paths must enforce TLS
    (minimum TLS 1.2; TLS 1.3 preferred). Unencrypted protocols (HTTP, FTP) must be
    disabled for resources handling personal data.
  - Key management: Customer-managed keys (CMKs) are preferred over provider-managed
    keys for sensitive personal data because CMKs provide stronger demonstrable control.
  - Key rotation must be enabled and configured for all CMKs.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 34 - COMMUNICATION OF BREACH TO DATA SUBJECT (ENCRYPTION AS MITIGANT)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 34(1):
  When the personal data breach is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and
  freedoms of natural persons, the controller shall communicate the personal data
  breach to the data subject without undue delay.
 
Article 34(3)(a) - Exemption from Notification:
  The communication to the data subject referred to in paragraph 1 shall not be
  required if any of the following conditions are met:
  (a) the controller has implemented appropriate technical and organisational protection
      measures, and those measures were applied to the personal data affected by the
      personal data breach, in particular those that render the personal data unintelligible
      to any person who is not authorised to access it, such as encryption.
 
Practical Implication:
  If personal data is properly encrypted at rest and the encryption keys were not
  compromised in a breach, the controller may be exempt from the obligation to notify
  individual data subjects. This provides a direct operational incentive for strong
  encryption practices.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - Strong encryption with separate key management is a breach notification risk mitigant.
  - If an S3 bucket is exposed but all objects are AES-256 encrypted with KMS CMKs that
    were not compromised, breach notification to individuals may not be required.
  - This makes encryption with proper key separation a materially important control.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 33 - NOTIFICATION OF BREACH TO SUPERVISORY AUTHORITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 33(3)(d):
  The notification of a personal data breach to the supervisory authority shall at least
  include a description of the measures taken or proposed to be taken by the controller
  to address the personal data breach, including, where appropriate, measures to mitigate
  its possible adverse effects.
 
Practical Implication:
  The presence of encryption must be documented and reported as a mitigating measure
  when notifying the supervisory authority of a breach. The notification assessment
  must include whether encrypted data was involved and whether keys were exposed.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 89 - SAFEGUARDS FOR ARCHIVING AND RESEARCH PURPOSES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 89(1):
  Processing for archiving purposes in the public interest, scientific or historical
  research purposes or statistical purposes shall be subject to appropriate safeguards,
  in accordance with this Regulation, for the rights and freedoms of the data subject.
  Those safeguards shall ensure that technical and organisational measures are in place
  in particular in order to ensure respect for the principle of data minimisation. Those
  measures may include pseudonymisation provided that those purposes can be fulfilled
  in that manner.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - Long-term archival storage (S3 Glacier, Azure Archive, GCS Archive) for personal
    data must still have encryption enabled.
  - Archived personal data should use CMKs with lifecycle policies that can revoke
    access when retention periods expire.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PRACTICAL CLOUD ENCRYPTION CONTROLS DERIVED FROM GDPR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
1. Object Storage (S3 / Blob Storage / GCS):
   - Enable default server-side encryption on all buckets/containers.
   - Use customer-managed keys (AWS KMS CMK, Azure Key Vault key, GCP CMEK) for
     personal data.
   - Enable bucket keys (S3) to reduce KMS call costs while maintaining CMK protection.
   - Enforce HTTPS-only access; disable HTTP.
   - Set minimum TLS version to TLS 1.2 or higher.
 
2. Relational Databases (RDS / Azure PostgreSQL / Cloud SQL):
   - Enable encryption at rest; this covers data files, logs, backups, snapshots, and
     replicas.
   - Enable SSL/TLS enforcement for all client connections; reject unencrypted connections.
   - Use CMKs where the sensitivity of data justifies the operational overhead.
   - For RDS: enable Performance Insights encryption and automated backup encryption.
 
3. Compute (EC2 / VMs / Compute Engine):
   - Encrypt all EBS volumes / managed disks / persistent disks attached to instances
     that process personal data.
   - Use encrypted AMIs / images.
   - Do not store personal data in unencrypted instance storage (ephemeral disks).
 
4. Serverless / Functions:
   - Ensure environment variables containing personal data or credentials are encrypted
     at rest using CMKs.
   - Use encrypted layers and deployment packages where personal data might be embedded.
 
5. CDN / Network Transfer (CloudFront / Azure CDN / Cloud CDN):
   - Enforce HTTPS for all viewer connections; redirect HTTP to HTTPS.
   - Set minimum TLS version to TLS 1.2.
   - Disable outdated cipher suites that do not provide forward secrecy.
 
6. Key Management Best Practices:
   - Enable automatic key rotation (annually as minimum; more frequently for high-risk data).
   - Restrict key usage policies to only the services and roles that require them.
   - Enable key deletion protection; require a minimum waiting period before key deletion.
   - Store encryption keys in a hardware security module (HSM)-backed service where
     the sensitivity of data justifies it.
   - Never store encryption keys in the same location as the encrypted data.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PENALTIES FOR NON-COMPLIANCE WITH ENCRYPTION OBLIGATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 83(4):
  Infringements of Article 25 (data protection by design) and Article 32 (security
  of processing, including encryption) are subject to administrative fines of up to
  EUR 10,000,000 or 2% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher.
 
Supervisory Authority Guidance:
  Several EU Data Protection Authorities have issued enforcement decisions specifically
  citing the failure to implement adequate encryption as a violation of Article 32.
  Notable cases include fines for storing passwords in plaintext, transmitting personal
  data over unencrypted connections, and failing to encrypt backup media.
