GDPR — DATA RETENTION
================================================================================
 
OVERVIEW
--------
Data retention under the GDPR is governed primarily by the storage limitation
principle in Article 5(1)(e) and the right to erasure in Article 17. Together,
these provisions require organizations to:
 
1. Keep personal data only for as long as it is necessary for the purpose for
   which it was collected.
2. Define and document retention periods before or at the time of collection.
3. Securely delete or effectively anonymise personal data when the retention
   period expires.
4. Apply the same retention obligations to all copies of data — including
   backups, archives, and cloud snapshots.
 
The GDPR does not specify fixed retention durations for most categories of
personal data. Instead, it requires organizations to justify each retention
period based on the purpose of processing, applicable legal or regulatory
obligations, and the principle of data minimisation.
 
Relevant articles:
 
  - Article 5(1)(b): Purpose limitation
  - Article 5(1)(c): Data minimisation
  - Article 5(1)(e): Storage limitation
  - Article 5(2):    Accountability — demonstrate retention decisions
  - Article 6:       Lawfulness of processing — basis for retention
  - Article 13–14:   Transparency — inform subjects of retention periods
  - Article 17:      Right to erasure
  - Article 30:      Records of processing — document retention periods
  - Article 89:      Derogations for archiving, research, statistics
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 5(1)(e) — STORAGE LIMITATION PRINCIPLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text: Personal data shall be "kept in a form which permits identification
of data subjects for no longer than is necessary for the purposes for which the
personal data are processed; personal data may be stored for longer periods
insofar as the personal data will be processed solely for archiving purposes in
the public interest, scientific or historical research purposes or statistical
purposes in accordance with Article 89(1) subject to implementation of the
appropriate technical and organisational measures required by this Regulation
in order to safeguard the rights and freedoms of the data subject
('storage limitation')."
 
This is the core GDPR data retention obligation. Key principles derived from it:
 
1. TIME-BOUND BY PURPOSE
   Data must only be held for as long as it serves the purpose for which it was
   collected. Once the purpose is fulfilled, the legal basis for retention ends
   and the data must be deleted or anonymised.
 
2. NO INDEFINITE RETENTION
   Keeping personal data indefinitely without a specific, documented justification
   is non-compliant. The absence of a retention schedule is itself a GDPR
   violation under the accountability principle (Article 5(2)).
 
3. IDENTIFICATION AS THE THRESHOLD
   The storage limitation applies to data "which permits identification of data
   subjects." This means:
   - Truly anonymised data (where re-identification is impossible) falls outside
     GDPR scope.
   - Pseudonymised data (e.g., records linked to a tokenized ID) still permits
     identification and remains within GDPR scope.
   - Archived or backed-up data containing identifiable personal data remains
     subject to the storage limitation.
 
4. ARCHIVING EXCEPTION
   The storage limitation does not apply to data retained for archiving purposes
   in the public interest, scientific or historical research, or statistical
   purposes — provided:
   - Appropriate safeguards are implemented (Article 89(1))
   - The data is not used for other purposes
   - Technical measures minimize the risk to data subjects (e.g., pseudonymisation)
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 5(1)(b) — PURPOSE LIMITATION AND RETENTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text: Personal data shall be "collected for specified, explicit and legitimate
purposes and not further processed in a manner that is incompatible with those
purposes."
 
Purpose limitation intersects directly with retention: if data is being retained
beyond the period needed for its original purpose, continuing to hold it requires
either a compatible new purpose or a new lawful basis. Retention purely because
the data "might be useful one day" is not a valid purpose.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 5(1)(c) — DATA MINIMISATION AND RETENTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text: Personal data shall be "adequate, relevant and limited to what is
necessary in relation to the purposes for which they are processed."
 
Data minimisation applies not only to what data is collected but to how long it
is held. Holding more data fields, or holding data for longer, than is necessary
for the processing purpose violates data minimisation. Organizations must
regularly review retained data and delete fields or records that are no longer
necessary.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 6 — LAWFUL BASIS AND ITS EFFECT ON RETENTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (summary): Processing is lawful only if at least one of the following
applies:
  (a) The data subject has given consent
  (b) Processing is necessary for the performance of a contract
  (c) Processing is necessary for compliance with a legal obligation
  (d) Processing is necessary to protect vital interests
  (e) Processing is necessary for a task in the public interest
  (f) Processing is necessary for the legitimate interests of the controller
 
The lawful basis chosen for processing determines the retention period:
 
  CONSENT (6(1)(a)):
    Retention is valid only as long as consent remains in force. When consent is
    withdrawn, the lawful basis ends and the data must be deleted unless another
    basis applies. Consent records themselves should be retained to demonstrate
    that consent was valid during the period of processing.
 
  CONTRACT (6(1)(b)):
    Data collected for contract performance can be retained for the duration of
    the contract and for a reasonable period thereafter to handle post-contract
    queries, disputes, or legal claims. Common retention period: contract
    duration plus applicable statute of limitations (varies by jurisdiction).
 
  LEGAL OBLIGATION (6(1)(c)):
    Specific legal or regulatory requirements may mandate minimum retention
    periods (e.g., tax records 6–7 years, employment records 6 years in the UK).
    These obligations override the storage limitation for the required period
    but do not justify retention beyond it.
 
  LEGITIMATE INTERESTS (6(1)(f)):
    Retention must be no longer than is necessary for the legitimate interest.
    Balancing test results and retention justification should be documented.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 13 AND 14 — TRANSPARENCY: INFORMING DATA SUBJECTS OF RETENTION PERIODS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (Article 13(2)(a)): At the time personal data are collected, the
controller shall provide the data subject with "the period for which the personal
data will be stored, or if that is not possible, the criteria used to determine
that period."
 
Full text (Article 14(3)(a)): The same obligation applies where data was not
obtained directly from the data subject.
 
Implications:
1. Retention periods must be determined before data is collected — not after.
2. Where a specific period cannot be stated, the criteria for determining the
   period must be explained (e.g., "retained for the duration of the contract
   plus 6 years").
3. Privacy notices and consent forms must reflect the actual retention period.
4. If the retention period changes, data subjects must be informed.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 17 — RIGHT TO ERASURE ('RIGHT TO BE FORGOTTEN')
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (paragraph 1): "The data subject shall have the right to obtain from
the controller the erasure of personal data concerning him or her without undue
delay and the controller shall have the obligation to erase personal data without
undue delay where one of the following grounds applies:
 
  (a) the personal data are no longer necessary in relation to the purposes
      for which they were collected or otherwise processed;
  (b) the data subject withdraws consent on which the processing is based and
      there is no other legal ground for the processing;
  (c) the data subject objects to the processing pursuant to Article 21(1) and
      there are no overriding legitimate grounds for the processing, or the
      data subject objects pursuant to Article 21(2);
  (d) the personal data have been unlawfully processed;
  (e) the personal data have to be erased for compliance with a legal obligation
      in Union or Member State law;
  (f) the personal data have been collected in relation to the offer of
      information society services referred to in Article 8(1)."
 
The right to erasure is not absolute. It may be overridden where processing is
necessary for:
  - Exercising freedom of expression and information (Article 17(3)(a))
  - Compliance with a legal obligation (Article 17(3)(b))
  - Public health purposes (Article 17(3)(c))
  - Archiving in the public interest, scientific/historical research, or
    statistics (Article 17(3)(d))
  - Establishment, exercise or defence of legal claims (Article 17(3)(e))
 
Erasure vs. anonymisation:
  - Where complete deletion is not feasible (e.g., data embedded in backup
    archives), effective anonymisation — removing all direct and indirect
    identifiers so re-identification is not possible — is an accepted alternative.
  - Pseudonymisation is NOT anonymisation: pseudonymised data remains personal
    data and is still subject to the right to erasure.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 30 — RECORDS OF PROCESSING — DOCUMENTING RETENTION PERIODS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (paragraph 1(f)): Records of processing activities shall include
"where possible, the envisaged time limits for erasure of different categories
of data."
 
This means:
  - The retention schedule for each data category must be documented in the
    Record of Processing Activities (RoPA).
  - This documentation must be available to the supervisory authority on request.
  - If erasure time limits cannot be specified precisely, the criteria for
    determining them must be stated.
 
A complete retention schedule in the RoPA is strong evidence that the
organization is managing personal data in a structured and accountable way.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 89 — DEROGATIONS FOR ARCHIVING, RESEARCH, AND STATISTICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (summary): Processing for archiving purposes in the public interest,
scientific or historical research purposes, or statistical purposes shall be
subject to appropriate safeguards (e.g., pseudonymisation). Member States may
provide further derogations from certain data subject rights in these contexts.
 
Conditions for invoking the Article 89 derogation:
  1. The primary purpose of retention must genuinely be archiving, research,
     or statistics — not general business purposes.
  2. Appropriate safeguards must be implemented (typically pseudonymisation
     and access controls).
  3. The derogation cannot be used as a general escape from the storage
     limitation principle.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ESTABLISHING A GDPR-COMPLIANT RETENTION SCHEDULE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
A compliant retention schedule must:
 
1. IDENTIFY ALL DATA CATEGORIES
   Map every category of personal data processed by the organization, including
   data held in cloud databases, object storage, backups, logs, and archives.
 
2. DETERMINE THE LAWFUL BASIS FOR EACH CATEGORY
   Document which Article 6 basis applies to each data category and how it
   affects the retention period.
 
3. IDENTIFY APPLICABLE LEGAL RETENTION REQUIREMENTS
   Some personal data is subject to mandatory minimum retention periods under
   sector-specific law (e.g., financial, employment, health). These override
   the storage limitation for the required minimum period.
 
4. SET DEFINED RETENTION PERIODS
   Specify a period for each data category. Where an exact period cannot be given,
   document the criteria that will be used to determine when data is deleted.
 
5. IMPLEMENT AUTOMATED DELETION OR ANONYMISATION
   Manual deletion processes are error-prone. Automated lifecycle policies should
   enforce deletion or anonymisation at the end of the retention period.
 
6. APPLY RETENTION TO ALL COPIES
   Retention periods apply equally to production data, backups, archives, logs,
   and cloud snapshots. Each must be included in the retention schedule.
 
7. REVIEW AND UPDATE REGULARLY
   Retention schedules should be reviewed at least annually and when processing
   purposes change.
 
8. DOCUMENT AND MAKE AVAILABLE
   The schedule must be recorded in the RoPA (Article 30) and reflected in
   privacy notices (Articles 13–14).

CLOUD CONFIGURATION IMPLICATIONS — SUMMARY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
GDPR retention obligations translate directly into the following cloud
configuration requirements:
 
1. OBJECT STORAGE LIFECYCLE POLICIES
   - AWS S3: Configure Lifecycle Rules to automatically expire objects after the
     defined retention period. Set expiration actions to permanently delete
     objects. Use Intelligent-Tiering for cost efficiency during the retention
     period where access frequency is variable.
   - Azure Blob Storage: Configure Blob Lifecycle Management policies with
     delete actions after defined number of days since last modification or
     creation. Enable immutability policies with defined retention intervals
     for compliance-locked data.
   - GCP Cloud Storage: Configure Object Lifecycle Management rules with
     Age condition and Delete action to enforce retention periods. Use
     retention policies on buckets to prevent deletion before minimum
     retention period expires.
 
2. DATABASE RETENTION
   - Implement application-level data purging routines aligned with the
     retention schedule.
   - For RDS/Cloud SQL/Azure SQL: implement scheduled jobs to delete expired
     personal data records.
   - Ensure purging logic covers all tables and fields holding personal data,
     not just primary user records.
   - Test purging routines and retain records of execution as audit evidence.
 
3. LOG RETENTION
   - Logs containing personal data (e.g., application logs with user identifiers,
     IP addresses, or behavioral data) are subject to GDPR retention limits.
   - Define log retention periods and configure CloudWatch, Azure Monitor, or
     GCP Cloud Logging accordingly.
   - Minimum retention for security audit logs: 12 months operational plus
     longer archival if required for breach evidence.
   - Logs should be reviewed for personal data content. Excessive personal data
     in logs (e.g., full request payloads containing user data) should be reduced
     at collection time through sanitization or redaction.
 
4. BACKUP RETENTION
   - Backup retention periods must be documented and enforced.
   - AWS Backup, Azure Backup, and GCP Backup policies should define retention
     periods aligned with the RoPA.
   - When a backup expires, the personal data in it must be deleted. This includes
     automated deletion of snapshot chains and backup vault entries.
 
5. ENCRYPTION AND KEY MANAGEMENT FOR RETENTION ENFORCEMENT
   - Cryptographic erasure: deleting the encryption key renders encrypted
     personal data irrecoverable and constitutes effective erasure where direct
     deletion is not feasible.
   - AWS KMS, Azure Key Vault, and GCP Cloud KMS support key deletion with
     configurable grace periods (minimum 7–30 days depending on provider).
   - Cryptographic erasure must be documented to serve as audit evidence that
     the data is no longer accessible.
 
6. AUDIT AND REPORTING
   - Maintain records of data deletions and lifecycle policy executions.
   - Generate periodic reports showing that retention schedules are being
     enforced in practice.
   - These reports are audit evidence under Article 5(2) accountability.

SPECIAL CATEGORIES OF DATA — HEIGHTENED RETENTION OBLIGATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Article 9 defines special categories of personal data requiring heightened
protection, including:
  - Health data
  - Genetic and biometric data
  - Racial or ethnic origin
  - Political opinions
  - Religious or philosophical beliefs
  - Trade union membership
  - Sexual orientation and sex life data
 
For special categories:
  - Retention periods should generally be shorter unless a specific legal
    obligation requires longer retention.
  - Deletion processes must be more rigorous and fully auditable.
  - Storage in cloud environments must be documented and assessed via DPIA
    (Article 35).
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PENALTIES FOR RETENTION VIOLATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Under Article 83(5), violations of the basic principles for processing, including
the storage limitation principle (Article 5), and violations of the right to
erasure (Article 17) can result in fines of up to:
  - EUR 20,000,000, or
  - 4% of total worldwide annual turnover of the preceding financial year,
    whichever is higher.
 
Under Article 83(4), violations of recordkeeping obligations (Article 30)
including failure to document retention periods can result in fines of up to:
  - EUR 10,000,000, or
  - 2% of total worldwide annual turnover,
    whichever is higher.
