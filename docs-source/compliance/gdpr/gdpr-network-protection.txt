================================================================================
GDPR NETWORK PROTECTION REQUIREMENTS
================================================================================
 
OVERVIEW
--------
The GDPR does not specify network architecture requirements directly but the
obligations under Article 5 (integrity and confidentiality), Article 25 (data
protection by design), Article 32 (security of processing), and Recital 49 (network
and information security as a legitimate interest) together impose network protection
obligations. In particular, protecting personal data from "unauthorised disclosure
of, or access to" personal data transmitted over networks is a core requirement.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 5 - INTEGRITY AND CONFIDENTIALITY PRINCIPLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 5(1)(f):
  Personal data shall be processed in a manner that ensures appropriate security of
  the personal data, including protection against unauthorised or unlawful processing
  and against accidental loss, destruction or damage, using appropriate technical or
  organisational measures ('integrity and confidentiality').
 
Practical Implication:
  Network-level protection is a technical measure that implements the integrity and
  confidentiality principle. Data transmitted over unsecured networks is vulnerable
  to interception (loss of confidentiality) and tampering (loss of integrity).
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
RECITAL 49 - NETWORK AND INFORMATION SECURITY AS LEGITIMATE INTEREST
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Recital 49:
  The processing of personal data to the extent strictly necessary and proportionate
  for the purposes of ensuring network and information security, i.e. the ability of
  a network or an information system to resist, at a given level of confidence,
  accidental events or unlawful or malicious actions that compromise the availability,
  authenticity, integrity and confidentiality of stored or transmitted personal data,
  and the security of the related services offered by, or accessible via, those
  networks and systems, by public authorities, by computer emergency response teams
  (CERTs), computer security incident response teams (CSIRTs), by providers of
  electronic communications networks and services and by providers of security
  technologies and services, constitutes a legitimate interest of the data controller
  concerned. This could, for example, include preventing unauthorised access to
  electronic communications networks and malicious code distribution and stopping
  'denial of service' attacks and damage to computer and electronic communication
  systems.
 
Practical Implication:
  Recital 49 explicitly recognises that running firewalls, intrusion detection systems,
  DDoS protection, and network monitoring is not only permitted but is itself a
  legitimate interest. This means that network security tooling does not require a
  separate consent basis when it processes personal data incidentally (e.g., logging
  IP addresses for security purposes).
 
Cloud Implication:
  - WAF (Web Application Firewall) deployment, DDoS protection (AWS Shield, Azure DDoS
    Protection, GCP Cloud Armor), and network flow logging are all justified and
    expected under GDPR.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 25 - DATA PROTECTION BY DESIGN (NETWORK ARCHITECTURE)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 25(1):
  The controller shall implement appropriate technical and organisational measures,
  such as pseudonymisation, which are designed to implement data-protection principles
  in an effective manner and to integrate the necessary safeguards into the processing.
 
Article 25(2):
  The controller shall ensure that by default personal data are not made accessible
  without the individual's intervention to an indefinite number of natural persons.
 
Practical Implication:
  By default, services handling personal data must not be exposed to the public
  internet. Network segmentation and private networking must be the default design,
  not an afterthought.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - Databases must not have public endpoints enabled.
  - Application tiers must be in private subnets.
  - Public internet exposure must be limited to intended public-facing services only.
  - Private endpoints/service endpoints must be used for cloud service access within
    a VPC/VNet.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 32 - SECURITY OF PROCESSING (NETWORK PROTECTION OBLIGATIONS)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 32(1):
  The controller and the processor shall implement appropriate technical and
  organisational measures to ensure a level of security appropriate to the risk,
  including as appropriate:
  (a) the pseudonymisation and encryption of personal data;
  (b) the ability to ensure the ongoing confidentiality, integrity, availability and
      resilience of processing systems and services;
  (c) the ability to restore the availability and access to personal data in a timely
      manner in the event of a physical or technical incident.
 
Article 32(2):
  In assessing the appropriate level of security, account shall be taken in particular
  of the risks that are presented by processing, in particular from accidental or
  unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorised disclosure of, or access to,
  personal data transmitted, stored or otherwise processed.
 
The phrase "transmitted, stored or otherwise processed" explicitly covers data in
transit over networks. Protection of personal data during transmission is a direct
requirement of Article 32.
 
Recital 83:
  The controller or processor should evaluate the risks inherent in the processing
  and implement measures to mitigate those risks, such as encryption. Those measures
  should ensure an appropriate level of security, including confidentiality, taking
  into account the state of the art and the costs of implementation.
 
"State of the art" for network protection includes TLS 1.2/1.3 for all transmissions,
WAF for web-facing services, private networking where feasible, and network-level
threat detection.
 
Cloud Implication - Encryption in Transit:
  - All connections to and from services handling personal data must use TLS.
  - Minimum TLS version: TLS 1.2. TLS 1.3 preferred.
  - SSL/TLS certificates must be valid, not self-signed (except for internal services
    where certificate validation is still enforced), and renewed before expiry.
  - HTTP access must be redirected to HTTPS or rejected entirely.
 
Cloud Implication - Network Segmentation:
  - Use VPC / VNet to isolate personal data workloads from other workloads.
  - Security groups / NSGs must follow least-privilege ingress and egress rules.
  - Do not use 0.0.0.0/0 as an inbound rule for resources handling personal data.
  - Use private subnets for databases and application servers; use public subnets only
    for load balancers and CDN origins.
  - Use NACLs (AWS) or subnet-level controls as a defence-in-depth layer.
 
Cloud Implication - Private Endpoints:
  - Use VPC Endpoints (AWS), Private Endpoints (Azure), or Private Service Connect
    (GCP) for cloud service access to avoid traffic traversing the public internet.
  - This applies to: object storage, databases, KMS, secrets management, and any other
    service that handles personal data.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 32 - AVAILABILITY AND RESILIENCE (NETWORK AVAILABILITY)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 32(1)(b):
  the ability to ensure the ongoing confidentiality, integrity, availability and
  resilience of processing systems and services.
 
Article 32(1)(c):
  the ability to restore the availability and access to personal data in a timely
  manner in the event of a physical or technical incident.
 
Practical Implication:
  Network resilience is a GDPR obligation. DDoS attacks and network outages are
  "physical or technical incidents" that must be accounted for. Data subjects have
  rights of access (Article 15) and rectification (Article 16), and controllers must
  be able to fulfill these even in the event of attacks.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - Enable DDoS protection at the network level (AWS Shield Standard minimum; Shield
    Advanced for critical applications, Azure DDoS Protection, GCP Cloud Armor).
  - Use multi-AZ or multi-region deployment where personal data availability is critical.
  - Configure health checks and auto-scaling to maintain availability.
  - CDN (CloudFront, Azure CDN, GCP Cloud CDN) can help absorb traffic and reduce
    exposure of origin services.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 44-49 - INTERNATIONAL DATA TRANSFERS (NETWORK IMPLICATION)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 44:
  Any transfer of personal data to a third country or an international organisation
  shall take place only if the conditions laid down in this Chapter are complied with
  by the controller and processor.
 
Article 46(1):
  In the absence of a decision pursuant to Article 45(3), a controller or processor
  may transfer personal data to a third country or an international organisation only
  if the controller or processor has provided appropriate safeguards, and on condition
  that enforceable data subject rights and effective legal remedies for data subjects
  are available.
 
Practical Implication:
  Any personal data that traverses a network outside the EU/EEA is potentially a
  cross-border transfer subject to Chapter V of the GDPR. Network routing must be
  considered when designing data flows.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - If personal data of EU residents is processed, cloud regions used must be
    documented and cross-border transfers must be covered by appropriate safeguards
    (Standard Contractual Clauses, Binding Corporate Rules, or adequacy decisions).
  - Data residency settings on cloud services (S3 bucket region, Azure region, GCP
    region) must be configured to comply with transfer restrictions.
  - CDN edge caching must be evaluated: caching personal data at edge nodes in third
    countries may constitute a transfer.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PRACTICAL CLOUD NETWORK CONTROLS DERIVED FROM GDPR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
1. Encryption in Transit:
   - Enforce HTTPS/TLS for all public-facing endpoints and APIs.
   - Enforce TLS for internal service-to-service communication where personal data is
     transmitted.
   - Configure minimum TLS version to TLS 1.2 (enforce TLS 1.3 where supported).
   - Disable SSLv3, TLS 1.0, and TLS 1.1.
   - Use valid, CA-signed certificates; implement automated certificate renewal.
 
2. Network Segmentation:
   - Use separate VPCs/VNets for different workload tiers.
   - Deploy databases and application servers in private subnets.
   - Use load balancers and CDN as the public internet boundary.
   - Restrict security group / NSG rules: no 0.0.0.0/0 inbound on port 22 (SSH) or
     3389 (RDP) for production systems.
   - Use bastion hosts or session manager for administrative access instead of
     direct internet-exposed SSH/RDP.
 
3. Private Connectivity:
   - Use VPC Endpoints / Private Endpoints for all cloud services that handle personal
     data to avoid public internet traversal.
   - Use PrivateLink / Private Service Connect for cross-VPC service access.
 
4. DDoS and WAF:
   - Enable WAF in front of all web applications and APIs that handle personal data.
   - Configure WAF rules to block common attacks (OWASP Top 10).
   - Enable DDoS protection at the network and application layer.
 
5. Network Monitoring:
   - Enable VPC Flow Logs / NSG Flow Logs / GCP VPC Flow Logs.
   - Monitor for unusual egress patterns that may indicate data exfiltration.
   - Alert on security group / firewall rule changes.
 
6. Data Residency:
   - Configure cloud resources in approved regions.
   - Restrict resource creation to approved regions using organisation policies /
     SCPs / constraints.
   - Audit cross-region data replication to ensure transfers are covered by safeguards.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PENALTIES FOR NON-COMPLIANCE WITH NETWORK PROTECTION OBLIGATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 83(4):
  Infringements of Article 25 and Article 32 (including network protection failures
  that expose personal data) are subject to fines up to EUR 10,000,000 or 2% of
  global annual turnover.
 
Article 83(5):
  Infringements of the basic principles including integrity and confidentiality
  (Article 5(1)(f)) are subject to fines up to EUR 20,000,000 or 4% of global annual
  turnover.
 
Supervisory Authority Guidance:
  Multiple DPA enforcement actions have resulted from failures to encrypt data in
  transit (e.g., transmitting personal data over HTTP). Unencrypted network
  transmission of personal data has been consistently treated as a violation of
  Article 32(1)(a) and Article 5(1)(f).
