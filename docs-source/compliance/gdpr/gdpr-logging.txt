GDPR LOGGING AND MONITORING REQUIREMENTS
================================================================================
 
OVERVIEW
--------
The GDPR establishes logging and monitoring obligations through several mechanisms:
the accountability principle (Article 5(2)), the obligation to maintain records of
processing activities (Article 30), breach detection and notification requirements
(Articles 33 and 34), and the general security of processing obligation (Article 32).
The regulation does not specify minimum log retention periods for most purposes, but
imposes obligations that are practically impossible to fulfill without robust logging.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 5(2) - ACCOUNTABILITY PRINCIPLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 5(2):
  The controller shall be responsible for, and be able to demonstrate compliance with,
  paragraph 1 ('accountability').
 
Paragraph 1 includes the principles of lawfulness, fairness, transparency, purpose
limitation, data minimisation, accuracy, storage limitation, and integrity and
confidentiality.
 
Practical Implication:
  Demonstrating compliance with each principle requires evidence. Logs of who accessed
  personal data, when, from where, and what was done are the primary evidence base
  for demonstrating compliance. Without logs, the controller cannot demonstrate that
  personal data was only accessed by authorized persons for stated purposes.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - Enable CloudTrail (AWS), Azure Monitor / Activity Log (Azure), and Cloud Audit Logs
    (GCP) across all accounts and projects that handle personal data.
  - These logs are the accountability evidence required by Article 5(2).

ARTICLE 30 - RECORDS OF PROCESSING ACTIVITIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 30(1):
  Each controller and, where applicable, the controller's representative, shall maintain
  a record of processing activities under its responsibility. That record shall contain
  all of the following information:
  (a) the name and contact details of the controller;
  (b) the purposes of the processing;
  (c) a description of the categories of data subjects and of the categories of
      personal data;
  (d) the categories of recipients to whom the personal data have been or will be
      disclosed;
  (e) where applicable, transfers of personal data to a third country or an
      international organisation;
  (f) where possible, the envisaged time limits for erasure of the different categories
      of data;
  (g) where possible, a general description of the technical and organisational security
      measures referred to in Article 32(1).
 
Article 30(2):
  Each processor and, where applicable, the processor's representative, shall maintain
  a record of all categories of processing activities carried out on behalf of a
  controller, containing:
  (a) the name and contact details of the processor(s) and of each controller on whose
      behalf the processor is acting;
  (b) the categories of processing carried out on behalf of each controller;
  (c) where applicable, transfers of personal data to a third country or an
      international organisation;
  (d) where possible, a general description of the technical and organisational security
      measures referred to in Article 32(1).
 
Article 30(4):
  The controller or the processor and, where applicable, the controller's or the
  processor's representative, shall make the record available to the supervisory
  authority on request.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - The records of processing activities (RoPA) must reference the cloud services used
    for each processing activity.
  - Cloud resource configuration (enabled logging, encryption, access controls) must be
    documented in the general description of security measures under Article 30(1)(g).
  - Service configuration logs (CloudTrail, Activity Logs) provide evidence that security
    measures are actually implemented as described in the RoPA.

ARTICLE 32 - SECURITY OF PROCESSING (MONITORING COMPONENT)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 32(1)(d):
  The controller and the processor shall implement appropriate technical and
  organisational measures to ensure a level of security appropriate to the risk,
  including as appropriate:
  (d) a process for regularly testing, assessing and evaluating the effectiveness of
      technical and organisational measures for ensuring the security of the processing.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - Logging must be actively monitored, not just collected. Logs that are never reviewed
    do not satisfy Article 32(1)(d).
  - Anomaly detection (AWS GuardDuty, Azure Defender, GCP Security Command Center) should
    be enabled to provide automated monitoring over log data.
  - Regular access reviews and log reviews must be scheduled and documented.
  - CloudWatch Alarms, Azure Monitor Alerts, or GCP alerting policies should be
    configured to detect unauthorized access attempts.
 
Article 32(2):
  In assessing the appropriate level of security, account shall be taken in particular
  of the risks that are presented by processing, in particular from accidental or
  unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorised disclosure of, or access to,
  personal data transmitted, stored or otherwise processed.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - Log coverage must extend to all data access paths including API access, console
    access, programmatic access, and inter-service access.
  - Logs must capture both successful and failed access attempts.

ARTICLE 33 - NOTIFICATION OF BREACH TO SUPERVISORY AUTHORITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 33(1):
  In the case of a personal data breach, the controller shall without undue delay and,
  where feasible, not later than 72 hours after having become aware of it, notify the
  personal data breach to the competent supervisory authority, unless the personal data
  breach is unlikely to result in a risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons.
 
Article 33(2):
  The processor shall notify the controller without undue delay after becoming aware of
  a personal data breach.
 
Article 33(3):
  The notification to the supervisory authority shall at least:
  (a) describe the nature of the personal data breach including where possible, the
      categories and approximate number of data subjects concerned and the categories
      and approximate number of personal data records concerned;
  (b) communicate the name and contact details of the data protection officer;
  (c) describe the likely consequences of the personal data breach;
  (d) describe the measures taken or proposed to be taken by the controller to address
      the personal data breach, including, where appropriate, measures to mitigate its
      possible adverse effects.
 
Article 33(5):
  The controller shall document any personal data breaches, comprising the facts
  relating to the personal data breach, its effects and the remedial action taken. That
  documentation shall enable the supervisory authority to verify compliance with this
  Article.
 
Recital 85:
  A personal data breach may, if not addressed in an appropriate and timely manner,
  result in physical, material or non-material damage to natural persons such as loss
  of control over their personal data or limitation of their rights, discrimination,
  identity theft or fraud, financial loss, unauthorised reversal of pseudonymisation,
  damage to reputation, loss of confidentiality of personal data protected by
  professional secrecy or any other significant economic or social disadvantage.
  Therefore, as soon as the controller becomes aware that a personal data breach has
  occurred, the controller should notify the personal data breach to the supervisory
  authority without undue delay and, where feasible, not later than 72 hours after
  having become aware of it.
 
Recital 87:
  It should be ascertained whether all appropriate technological protection and
  organisational measures have been implemented to establish immediately whether a
  personal data breach has taken place and to inform promptly the supervisory authority
  and the data subject.
 
Practical Implication:
  The 72-hour notification window begins from the moment the controller "becomes aware."
  This means logging and alerting systems must be in place to detect breaches quickly.
  A controller with no logging cannot become aware of a breach promptly and therefore
  cannot meet the 72-hour requirement.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - Enable real-time alerting on unauthorized access patterns, unusual data egress, and
    permission changes affecting personal data storage.
  - AWS: CloudTrail + CloudWatch Alarms + GuardDuty + Security Hub.
  - Azure: Activity Log + Azure Monitor + Microsoft Defender for Cloud.
  - GCP: Cloud Audit Logs + Cloud Monitoring + Security Command Center.
  - Log exports must be retained for long enough to support post-incident investigation
    and documentation required by Article 33(5).

ARTICLE 58 - SUPERVISORY AUTHORITY INVESTIGATIVE POWERS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 58(1):
  Each supervisory authority shall have all of the following investigative powers:
  (a) to order the controller and the processor, and, where applicable, the
      controller's or the processor's representative to provide any information it
      requires for the performance of its tasks;
  (b) to carry out investigations in the form of data protection audits;
  (c) to carry out a review on certifications issued pursuant to Article 42(7);
  (d) to notify the controller or the processor of an alleged infringement of this
      Regulation;
  (e) to obtain, from the controller and the processor, access to all personal data
      and to all information necessary for the performance of its tasks;
  (f) to obtain access to any premises of the controller and the processor, including
      to any data processing equipment and means, in accordance with Union or Member
      State procedural law.
 
Practical Implication:
  Supervisory authorities have the right to audit logs during investigations. Absent or
  incomplete logs constitute both a failure to demonstrate accountability and potentially
  evidence of concealment, which can significantly increase the severity of any fine.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - Logs must be stored in tamper-resistant, centralized locations.
  - Log storage should have Object Lock / immutability features enabled (S3 Object Lock,
    Azure immutable blob storage, GCS retention policies).
  - Log access should be restricted: only security/compliance roles should be able to
    delete or modify log archives.
  - Logs must cover a time window sufficient to support regulatory investigation
    (typically 12 months minimum is considered reasonable; some DPAs expect longer).
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PRACTICAL CLOUD LOGGING CONTROLS DERIVED FROM GDPR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
1. Management / Control Plane Logging:
   - AWS: Enable CloudTrail in all regions; enable multi-region trail; enable log file
     validation; store in a dedicated, write-protected S3 bucket.
   - Azure: Enable Activity Log export; send to Log Analytics workspace or storage
     account with immutability policies.
   - GCP: Ensure Admin Activity audit logs are always on; enable Data Access audit logs
     for services handling personal data.
 
2. Data Plane Logging (Access to Personal Data):
   - AWS S3: Enable S3 Server Access Logging and/or S3 Event Notifications to record
     object-level access to buckets containing personal data.
   - AWS RDS: Enable database activity streams or enhanced monitoring and audit logging.
   - Azure: Enable Diagnostic Settings on Storage Accounts and Databases; send to
     Log Analytics.
   - GCP: Enable Data Access audit logs at the project and resource level.
 
3. Network Logging:
   - AWS: Enable VPC Flow Logs for VPCs hosting personal data workloads.
   - Azure: Enable NSG Flow Logs and Azure Network Watcher.
   - GCP: Enable VPC Flow Logs for subnets hosting personal data workloads.
 
4. Log Retention:
   - Retain logs for a minimum of 12 months to support breach investigation and
     supervisory authority audits. Many DPAs consider this a reasonable minimum.
   - Use tiered storage (hot + cold) to manage cost while maintaining accessibility.
   - Enable log immutability to prevent tampering.
 
5. Log Monitoring and Alerting:
   - Configure alerts for: unusual data volumes accessed, cross-region access, access
     from unexpected IPs, privilege escalation events, changes to security group or
     firewall rules, and disabling of logging itself.
   - Review alerts on a documented schedule and maintain evidence of reviews.
 
6. Documentation for Article 33(5):
   - Maintain an incident register documenting all personal data breaches, even those
     not reportable, with facts, effects, and remedial actions.
   - This register must be producible to the supervisory authority on request.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PENALTIES FOR NON-COMPLIANCE WITH LOGGING OBLIGATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 83(4):
  Failure to implement appropriate security measures including monitoring and incident
  detection (Article 32) is subject to fines up to EUR 10,000,000 or 2% of global
  annual turnover.
 
Article 83(5):
  Failure to demonstrate accountability (Article 5(2)) is subject to fines up to
  EUR 20,000,000 or 4% of global annual turnover.
 
Failure to document breaches as required by Article 33(5) is an infringement of
Article 33 and subject to fines up to EUR 10,000,000 or 2% of global annual turnover.
