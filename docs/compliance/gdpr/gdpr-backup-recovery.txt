GDPR — BACKUP AND RECOVERY
================================================================================
 
OVERVIEW
--------
The GDPR does not use the words "backup" or "disaster recovery" explicitly.
However, the obligation to maintain backup and recovery capabilities for personal
data is clearly and directly derived from several core provisions of the regulation.
The primary basis is Article 32, which requires organizations to ensure the
ongoing availability and resilience of personal data processing systems and to
implement the ability to restore availability and access to personal data in a
timely manner after a physical or technical incident.
 
Failure to maintain adequate backup and recovery for personal data is a failure
of the security of processing obligation under Article 32. It may also constitute
a violation of the integrity and confidentiality principle in Article 5(1)(f).
 
Relevant articles:
 
  - Article 5(1)(f):  Integrity and confidentiality principle
  - Article 17:       Right to erasure — implications for backed-up data
  - Article 25:       Data protection by design and by default
  - Article 32:       Security of processing — availability and resilience
  - Article 33:       Breach notification — loss of data as a notifiable breach
  - Article 83:       Administrative fines
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 5(1)(f) — INTEGRITY AND CONFIDENTIALITY PRINCIPLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text: Personal data shall be "processed in a manner that ensures appropriate
security of the personal data, including protection against unauthorised or unlawful
processing and against accidental loss, destruction or damage, using appropriate
technical or organisational measures ('integrity and confidentiality')."
 
This is one of the six core data protection principles in the GDPR. The phrase
"accidental loss, destruction or damage" directly establishes a legal obligation
to protect personal data from being irretrievably lost.
 
This means:
1. Personal data must not be stored in a way that creates a high risk of
   permanent loss in the event of a system failure, hardware fault, or
   natural disaster.
2. Backup mechanisms are a direct technical implementation of this principle.
3. The "appropriate" standard is risk-based: more sensitive data (e.g., health
   records, financial data) requires more robust backup controls than lower-risk
   data.
 
Cloud security implication: For any cloud service storing personal data,
enabling automated backups, versioning, and point-in-time recovery is a direct
implementation of Article 5(1)(f). Turning off backup features for cost savings
on services handling personal data may constitute a GDPR violation.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 32 — SECURITY OF PROCESSING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (paragraph 1): "Taking into account the state of the art, the costs
of implementation and the nature, scope, context and purposes of processing as
well as the risk of varying likelihood and severity for the rights and freedoms
of natural persons, the controller and the processor shall implement appropriate
technical and organisational measures to ensure a level of security appropriate
to the risk, including inter alia as appropriate:
 
  (a) the pseudonymisation and encryption of personal data;
  (b) the ability to ensure the ongoing confidentiality, integrity, availability
      and resilience of processing systems and services;
  (c) the ability to restore the availability and access to personal data in a
      timely manner in the event of a physical or technical incident;
  (d) a process for regularly testing, assessing and evaluating the effectiveness
      of technical and organisational measures for ensuring the security of the
      processing."
 
Article 32(1)(b) — Availability and Resilience:
This sub-clause establishes a specific obligation for availability. "Availability"
means personal data must be accessible when it is needed. "Resilience" means the
system must be able to withstand and recover from disruptions. Backup systems are
the primary technical mechanism for satisfying this requirement.
 
Article 32(1)(c) — Ability to Restore Availability and Access:
This is the most direct GDPR backup obligation. Organizations must have the
technical capability to restore access to personal data following a physical
or technical incident. Examples of such incidents include:
  - Hardware failure
  - Ransomware attacks
  - Accidental deletion of data
  - Cloud provider outages
  - Natural disasters affecting data center infrastructure
  - Software corruption or database failure
 
The phrase "in a timely manner" means the recovery capability must be active
and tested, not merely theoretical. An organization that has never tested its
recovery procedures cannot demonstrate this capability.
 
Article 32(1)(d) — Regular Testing:
Backup systems must be tested regularly. A backup that has never been tested
cannot be relied upon as a compliance measure. Testing evidence (restore test
logs, recovery time records) constitutes audit evidence under Article 30 and
the accountability principle in Article 5(2).
 
Full text (paragraph 2): "In assessing the appropriate level of security,
account shall be taken in particular of the risks that are presented by
processing, in particular from accidental or unlawful destruction, loss,
alteration, unauthorised disclosure of, or access to personal data transmitted,
stored or otherwise processed."
 
This paragraph confirms that accidental destruction and loss are among the
primary risks that security measures — including backups — must address.
 
Full text (paragraph 4): "The controller and processor shall take steps to
ensure that any natural person acting under the authority of the controller
or the processor who has access to personal data does not process them except
on instructions from the controller, unless he or she is required to do so
by Union or Member State law."
 
This clause also applies to backup systems: personnel with access to backup
data must be operating under defined instructions and must not process that
data beyond the purpose of backup and recovery.

ARTICLE 25 — DATA PROTECTION BY DESIGN AND BY DEFAULT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (summary): The controller shall implement appropriate technical and
organisational measures designed to implement data-protection principles
effectively and integrate the necessary safeguards into the processing.
 
Backup and recovery implications:
1. Recovery systems should be designed with data minimisation in mind — backup
   images should not retain personal data longer than the live system would.
2. Backup access controls should be as strict as production access controls —
   backups are not a lower-security copy of the data.
3. Encryption should be applied to backup data by default, not as an optional
   add-on.
4. Backup destinations (e.g., cross-region S3 buckets, Azure backup vaults, GCP
   backup storage) should be assessed for compliance with data residency
   requirements before being configured.

ARTICLE 17 — RIGHT TO ERASURE — IMPLICATIONS FOR BACKUP DATA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (paragraph 1): "The data subject shall have the right to obtain from
the controller the erasure of personal data concerning him or her without undue
delay" where one of specified grounds applies (e.g., data no longer necessary,
consent withdrawn, unlawful processing).
 
Backup data is fully within the scope of GDPR. Personal data in backup archives
and snapshots remains personal data to which GDPR obligations apply. This
has several critical implications for backup and recovery design:
 
1. ERASURE REQUESTS APPLY TO BACKUPS
   When a data subject invokes their Article 17 right to erasure, the controller
   must erase the personal data not only from live systems but also from backups,
   unless:
   - Erasure from backups is technically not feasible at the time of the request,
   - AND the controller documents why, AND commits to erasure when the backup is
     next accessed or rotated.
 
   The EDPB (European Data Protection Board) acknowledges that immediate erasure
   from backups may not always be possible. However, organizations must have a
   documented approach for handling erasure requests against backup data.
 
2. BACKUP RETENTION PERIODS MUST BE DEFINED AND JUSTIFIED
   Backups that retain personal data indefinitely create ongoing GDPR exposure.
   Backup retention periods must be set and enforced. When a backup lifecycle ends,
   personal data within it must be securely deleted or made effectively
   unidentifiable.
 
3. BACKUPS AS PROCESSORS
   If a third-party backup provider stores personal data, that provider is a
   processor under Article 28. A Data Processing Agreement must be in place
   before that provider receives any personal data. The DPA must specify:
   - What data the backup provider may access
   - Where it may be stored (geographic restrictions)
   - Retention periods
   - Security requirements
   - The provider's obligation to assist with erasure requests
 
4. CROSS-REGION AND CROSS-BORDER BACKUPS
   Backing up personal data of EU/EEA residents to cloud regions outside the EU/EEA
   constitutes an international data transfer under Chapter V of the GDPR. This is
   only permitted if:
   - The destination country has an adequacy decision (Article 45), OR
   - Appropriate safeguards are in place (e.g., Standard Contractual Clauses
     under Article 46), OR
   - Binding Corporate Rules (Article 47) apply.
 
   Cloud storage replication to US regions, for example, is subject to these
   transfer restrictions for EU personal data.

ARTICLE 33 — BREACH NOTIFICATION AND BACKUP RELEVANCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (paragraph 1): "In the case of a personal data breach, the controller
shall without undue delay and, where feasible, not later than 72 hours after
having become aware of it, notify the personal data breach to the supervisory
authority."
 
A personal data breach includes the accidental loss or destruction of personal
data (Article 4(12) definition: "a breach of security leading to the accidental
or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorised disclosure of, or access
to, personal data").
 
This means:
1. Loss of personal data because there was no backup, or a backup failed, can
   constitute a notifiable personal data breach if the loss is likely to result
   in a risk to the rights and freedoms of individuals.
2. A ransomware attack that encrypts personal data and destroys it is a breach
   requiring notification if the data cannot be restored from backup.
3. Accidental deletion of a database containing personal data is a breach if
   there is no backup enabling recovery.
 
Having functional, tested backups is therefore also a breach-prevention and
breach-mitigation measure. The presence of a working backup may reduce the
severity of an incident below the notification threshold.
 
Breach documentation (Article 33(5)): Even where notification is not required,
all incidents involving potential data loss must be documented in the breach
register. This includes incidents where backup recovery was successfully invoked.

SPECIFIC CLOUD BACKUP CONFIGURATION GUIDANCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The following cloud configurations directly satisfy GDPR backup and recovery
requirements under Articles 5(1)(f), 32(1)(b), and 32(1)(c):
 
AWS:
 
  S3:
    - Enable versioning: retains previous versions of objects, enabling recovery
      from accidental deletion or overwrite
    - Enable S3 Object Lock (WORM) for audit-critical data: prevents deletion
      during compliance retention period
    - Enable cross-region replication (CRR) for disaster recovery: data replicated
      to secondary region
    - Note: Cross-region replication to non-EU regions for EU personal data
      requires Article 46 safeguards
 
  RDS:
    - Enable automated backups with a retention period appropriate to recovery
      requirements (minimum 7 days recommended; up to 35 days supported)
    - Enable point-in-time recovery (PITR): allows database restore to any
      second within the retention window
    - Enable Multi-AZ deployment: automatic failover to standby instance on
      primary failure
    - Enable deletion protection: prevents accidental database deletion
 
  EC2/EBS:
    - Enable automated EBS snapshots via AWS Backup or Data Lifecycle Manager
    - Define snapshot retention policy aligned with backup retention schedule
    - Test restore from snapshot on a defined schedule (quarterly recommended)
 
  Encryption:
    - All backups must be encrypted at rest using KMS customer-managed keys (CMKs)
    - This satisfies Article 32(1)(a) and protects backed-up personal data
      from unauthorized access even if backup storage is compromised
 
Azure:
 
  Azure Backup Service:
    - Enable Azure Backup for all VMs, databases, and file shares containing
      personal data
    - Configure Recovery Services Vault with geo-redundant storage (GRS) for
      cross-region resilience
    - Apply Azure Backup policies with defined daily/weekly/monthly/annual
      retention periods
    - Enable soft delete: prevents backup data from being permanently deleted
      for a defined grace period after deletion request
 
  Azure SQL / PostgreSQL:
    - Automated backups are enabled by default: retain for 7–35 days
    - Enable geo-redundant backup for cross-region recovery capability
    - Test point-in-time restore procedures regularly
 
  Encryption:
    - Azure Backup encrypts all backup data using service-managed or
      customer-managed keys in Azure Key Vault
 
GCP:
 
  Cloud Storage:
    - Enable versioning: retains non-current versions of objects
    - Configure Object Lifecycle Management: defines retention and deletion
      schedules for backup objects
    - Use dual-region or multi-region buckets for resilience
 
  Cloud SQL:
    - Enable automated backups with defined retention (minimum 7 days)
    - Enable point-in-time recovery (PITR) using binary logging
    - Test instance clone/restore procedures regularly
 
  Compute Engine:
    - Enable persistent disk snapshots via Cloud Scheduler or Backup for GCE
    - Define snapshot retention lifecycle policies
 
  Encryption:
    - All Google-managed cloud storage is encrypted by default
    - Use Customer-Managed Encryption Keys (CMEK) for data requiring
      explicit key ownership under Article 32

BACKUP RETENTION POLICY REQUIREMENTS UNDER GDPR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
GDPR does not define specific backup retention periods. However:
 
1. Backup retention periods must be defined and documented.
2. They must be justified based on the recovery purpose and legal obligations.
3. They must not exceed what is necessary for the recovery purpose
   (storage limitation principle, Article 5(1)(e)).
4. When backup lifecycle ends, personal data must be securely and irreversibly
   deleted.
5. Backup retention periods for different data categories may differ based on
   the sensitivity and associated legal retention requirements.
 
Secure deletion at end of backup lifecycle:
  - Cryptographic erasure (deleting the encryption key) is an accepted method
    of rendering backed-up personal data inaccessible where direct deletion
    is not technically feasible.
  - This approach is particularly relevant for backup tapes, immutable storage,
    or cloud snapshot chains.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TESTING OBLIGATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Under Article 32(1)(d), backup and recovery procedures must be regularly tested.
 
Testing requirements:
  - Define a testing schedule (minimum annually; quarterly preferred for
    high-risk processing)
  - Document test methodology, date, scope, and outcome
  - Test full recovery, not just backup creation
  - Verify recovery time objective (RTO) and recovery point objective (RPO)
    meet operational and legal requirements
  - Retain test results as audit evidence
