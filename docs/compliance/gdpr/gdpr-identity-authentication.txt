GDPR IDENTITY AND AUTHENTICATION REQUIREMENTS
================================================================================
 
OVERVIEW
--------
The GDPR does not define specific authentication protocols but establishes obligations
that can only be satisfied through strong identity and authentication controls. The key
obligations are: ensuring only authorised persons can access personal data (Article 32),
applying data protection by default (Article 25), verifying the identity of data
subjects making access requests (Article 12), and demonstrating accountability
(Article 5(2)). Recital 64 explicitly addresses identity verification in the context
of online services.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 4 - DEFINITIONS (RELEVANT TO IDENTITY)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 4(1) - Personal Data:
  'personal data' means any information relating to an identified or identifiable
  natural person ('data subject'); an identifiable natural person is one who can be
  identified, directly or indirectly, in particular by reference to an identifier such
  as a name, an online identifier or to one or more factors specific to the physical,
  physiological, genetic, mental, economic, cultural or social identity of that natural
  person.
 
Article 4(5) - Pseudonymisation:
  'pseudonymisation' means the processing of personal data in such a manner that the
  personal data can no longer be attributed to a specific data subject without the use
  of additional information, provided that such additional information is kept separately
  and is subject to technical and organisational measures to ensure that the personal
  data are not attributed to an identified or identifiable natural person.
 
Note: Identifiers used for authentication (user IDs, session tokens, API keys) may
themselves constitute personal data if they can identify a natural person.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 5 - INTEGRITY AND CONFIDENTIALITY PRINCIPLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 5(1)(f):
  Personal data shall be processed in a manner that ensures appropriate security of
  the personal data, including protection against unauthorised or unlawful processing
  and against accidental loss, destruction or damage, using appropriate technical or
  organisational measures ('integrity and confidentiality').
 
Practical Implication:
  Authentication is the gateway to the "authorised" access requirement. Without strong
  authentication, the confidentiality and integrity principle cannot be met. Weak
  authentication (e.g., no MFA, default credentials, shared passwords) directly
  violates Article 5(1)(f).
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 25 - DATA PROTECTION BY DESIGN AND BY DEFAULT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 25(1):
  The controller shall implement appropriate technical and organisational measures,
  such as pseudonymisation, which are designed to implement data-protection principles
  in an effective manner and to integrate the necessary safeguards into the processing.
 
Article 25(2):
  The controller shall implement appropriate technical and organisational measures for
  ensuring that, by default, only personal data which are necessary for each specific
  purpose of the processing are processed. In particular, such measures shall ensure
  that by default personal data are not made accessible without the individual's
  intervention to an indefinite number of natural persons.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - IAM roles must follow least privilege as the default; overly broad roles must
    require explicit justification and approval.
  - Service accounts must not be assigned human-level permissions.
  - Root/owner accounts must not be used for routine operations; dedicated IAM
    users/roles must be created for all workloads.
  - MFA must be enforced by default for all human access to cloud management consoles
    and APIs that can reach personal data.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 29 - PROCESSING UNDER THE AUTHORITY OF THE CONTROLLER
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 29:
  The processor and any person acting under the authority of the controller or of the
  processor who has access to personal data shall not process those data except on
  instructions from the controller, unless required to do so by Union or Member State
  law.
 
Practical Implication:
  Only persons who are identified, authenticated, and acting under documented
  instructions may process personal data. Anonymous or shared-credential access to
  personal data is incompatible with this requirement because it is impossible to
  attribute processing actions to a specific natural person acting under instructions.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - No shared IAM users or shared credentials for accessing personal data.
  - Each person must have an individual identity with their own credentials.
  - Service-to-service authentication must use roles or managed identities, not shared
    static credentials.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 32 - SECURITY OF PROCESSING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 32(1):
  The controller and the processor shall implement appropriate technical and
  organisational measures to ensure a level of security appropriate to the risk,
  including as appropriate:
  (a) the pseudonymisation and encryption of personal data;
  (b) the ability to ensure the ongoing confidentiality, integrity, availability and
      resilience of processing systems and services.
 
Article 32(4):
  The controller and processor shall take steps to ensure that any natural person acting
  under the authority of the controller or the processor who has access to personal data
  does not process them except on instructions from the controller, unless required to
  do so by Union or Member State law.
 
Recital 83:
  The controller or processor should evaluate the risks inherent in the processing and
  implement measures to mitigate those risks, such as encryption. Those measures should
  ensure an appropriate level of security, including confidentiality, taking into account
  the state of the art.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - "State of the art" authentication for cloud environments means MFA using hardware
    tokens, authenticator apps (TOTP), or FIDO2/WebAuthn.
  - Password-only authentication is not considered "state of the art" for systems
    processing personal data under current EU supervisory authority guidance.
  - IAM password policies must enforce minimum length, complexity, and rotation.
  - Privileged access (admin roles) must require stronger authentication than standard
    roles (step-up MFA or just-in-time access).
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 12 - VERIFICATION OF DATA SUBJECT IDENTITY FOR ACCESS REQUESTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 12(1):
  The controller shall take appropriate measures to provide any information referred to
  in Articles 13 and 14 and any communication under Articles 15 to 22 and 34 relating
  to processing to the data subject in a concise, transparent, intelligible and easily
  accessible form, using clear and plain language.
 
Article 12(6):
  Without prejudice to Article 11, where the controller has reasonable doubts
  concerning the identity of the natural person making the request referred to in
  Articles 15 to 21, the controller may request the provision of additional information
  necessary to confirm the identity of the data subject.
 
Recital 64:
  The controller should use all reasonable measures to verify the identity of a data
  subject who requests access, in particular in the context of online services and
  online identifiers. A controller should not retain personal data for the sole purpose
  of being able to react to potential requests.
 
Practical Implication:
  Controllers must implement identity verification for data subject rights requests
  (Subject Access Requests). The verification method must be proportionate: over-
  collecting identity documents for low-risk requests may itself breach data minimisation.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - User-facing applications that handle Subject Access Requests must implement a
    secure, verifiable identity confirmation flow.
  - This flow must not collect more identity information than is necessary to confirm
    the data subject's identity for the specific request.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 17 - RIGHT TO ERASURE (IDENTITY IMPLICATION)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 17(1):
  The data subject shall have the right to obtain from the controller the erasure of
  personal data concerning him or her without undue delay.
 
Practical Implication:
  Executing erasure requires the ability to identify and locate all personal data
  belonging to a specific data subject across all systems. This requires that
  personal data be tagged to an identity and that identity management be consistent
  across systems.
 
Cloud Implication:
  - Data subject identifiers must be consistent across databases, object storage, and
    logs to enable comprehensive erasure.
  - Systems must be designed so that personal data can be found and deleted by
    data subject identifier without deleting data belonging to other subjects.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PRACTICAL CLOUD IDENTITY AND AUTHENTICATION CONTROLS DERIVED FROM GDPR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
1. IAM / Identity Management:
   - Enforce MFA for all human IAM users and root/owner accounts.
   - Disable or remove unused IAM users, roles, and service accounts.
   - Apply least-privilege role assignments; avoid Administrator/Owner roles for routine
     access.
   - Use IAM Access Analyzer (AWS), Azure AD Access Reviews, or GCP IAM Recommender to
     detect and remediate overly permissive access.
 
2. Service-to-Service Authentication:
   - Use IAM roles with instance profiles (AWS), managed identities (Azure), or
     service accounts with Workload Identity Federation (GCP) instead of static keys.
   - Rotate any static service account keys on a defined schedule.
   - Do not embed credentials in code, environment variables without encryption, or
     version control.
 
3. Human Access to Cloud Consoles:
   - Enforce MFA organisation-wide through AWS Organizations SCPs, Azure AD Conditional
     Access, or GCP Organisation Policy constraints.
   - Use Single Sign-On (SSO) with identity federation where possible to centralise
     identity lifecycle management.
   - Enforce strong password policies (minimum 12 characters; no common passwords).
 
4. Privileged Access:
   - Implement just-in-time (JIT) privileged access for production environments handling
     personal data.
   - Log all privileged access sessions.
   - Require manager approval for privilege escalation.
   - Revoke privileged access after the session.
 
5. Database Authentication:
   - Enable IAM authentication for databases (RDS IAM auth, Azure AD auth for PostgreSQL,
     Cloud SQL IAM auth).
   - Disable native database users where IAM authentication is available.
   - Rotate database master passwords on a defined schedule.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PENALTIES FOR NON-COMPLIANCE WITH IDENTITY AND AUTHENTICATION OBLIGATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Article 83(4):
  Infringements of Article 25 (data protection by design and by default) and Article 32
  (security of processing, including authentication) are subject to administrative fines
  of up to EUR 10,000,000 or 2% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher.
 
Article 83(5):
  Infringements of the basic principles including integrity and confidentiality
  (Article 5(1)(f)) are subject to fines up to EUR 20,000,000 or 4% of global annual
  turnover, whichever is higher.
