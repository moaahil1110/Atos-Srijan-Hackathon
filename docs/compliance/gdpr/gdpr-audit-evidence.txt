GDPR — AUDIT EVIDENCE AND ACCOUNTABILITY
================================================================================
 
OVERVIEW
--------
The GDPR places a strong, active obligation on data controllers and processors to
not only comply with the regulation but to be able to demonstrate that compliance
at any time — including during regulatory audits, investigations, and civil disputes.
This is known as the accountability principle. It is not sufficient to implement
controls silently; organizations must maintain documentary evidence that those
controls exist and function as intended.
 
The audit evidence requirements under GDPR are spread across several articles.
The most relevant are:
 
  - Article 5(2):   Accountability principle
  - Article 24:     Responsibility of the controller
  - Article 25:     Data protection by design and by default
  - Article 28:     Processor obligations and contracts
  - Article 30:     Records of processing activities (RoPA)
  - Article 32:     Security of processing and regular testing
  - Article 33(5):  Documentation of all personal data breaches
  - Article 35:     Data protection impact assessments (DPIAs)
  - Article 37–39:  Data Protection Officer (DPO) tasks and position
  - Article 58:     Supervisory authority investigative powers
 
Each of these articles generates a specific audit evidence obligation described
in detail below.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 5(2) — THE ACCOUNTABILITY PRINCIPLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text: "The controller shall be responsible for, and be able to demonstrate
compliance with, paragraph 1."
 
This is the foundational audit obligation in the GDPR. It means:
 
1. Compliance is the controller's active responsibility — not a passive state.
2. The controller must be able to demonstrate compliance upon request.
3. Demonstration normally requires written documentation, records, policies,
   and logs rather than oral assertions alone.
 
The accountability principle does not specify exactly how compliance must be
demonstrated. This is intentional: the required documentation varies depending
on the nature, scale, and risk level of the processing operation.
 
In most cases, compliance is demonstrated through a combination of:
  - Records of processing activities (Article 30)
  - Internal data protection policies
  - Data protection impact assessments (Article 35)
  - Processor contracts (Article 28)
  - Staff training records
  - Consent records (Article 7)
  - Security audit results (Article 32)
  - Breach logs (Article 33(5))
 
Cloud security implication: Any cloud service processing personal data on behalf
of an organization must support the controller's ability to demonstrate compliance.
Logging must be enabled. Access records must be maintained. Configuration changes
that affect data protection must be auditable. Cloud providers acting as processors
must cooperate with audits under Article 28.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 24 — RESPONSIBILITY OF THE CONTROLLER
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (summary): The controller shall implement appropriate technical and
organisational measures to ensure and be able to demonstrate that processing is
performed in accordance with the GDPR.
 
Key obligations for audit evidence:
 
1. Implement and maintain documented data protection policies.
2. Review and update those policies when necessary and proportionate.
3. Adhere to approved codes of conduct (Article 40) or certification mechanisms
   (Article 42) as a means of demonstrating compliance.
 
Article 24 makes clear that having documented measures is part of compliance
itself — not just evidence of it. A controller that cannot produce documentation
is non-compliant regardless of whether its processing is substantively correct.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 25 — DATA PROTECTION BY DESIGN AND BY DEFAULT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (summary): The controller shall implement appropriate technical and
organisational measures designed to implement data-protection principles in an
effective manner and to integrate the necessary safeguards into the processing.
 
Audit evidence this generates:
  - Design-time documentation showing that data minimisation was considered
  - Justification for each data field collected
  - Evidence that default settings are the most privacy-protective available
  - Records of architecture and configuration decisions at the system design phase
 
Cloud security implication: When recommending cloud configuration fields,
enabling privacy-protective defaults (e.g., block public access, disable
unauthenticated access, enforce encryption) is a direct expression of Article 25.
Documentation of why each default was chosen constitutes audit evidence.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 28 — PROCESSOR OBLIGATIONS AND CONTRACTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (summary): Where processing is to be carried out on behalf of a
controller, the controller shall use only processors providing sufficient
guarantees to implement appropriate technical and organisational measures.
 
Audit evidence this generates:
  - Written contracts or Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) with all processors
  - Evidence that the processor's compliance was assessed before engagement
  - Documentation of the scope of processing permitted
  - Sub-processor agreements and approval records
  - Audit rights clause in the DPA, and records of exercising those rights
 
Controllers are liable for processor non-compliance if they failed to perform
adequate due diligence. Evidence of due diligence (vendor assessments, contract
reviews, periodic audits) is therefore critical.
 
Cloud security implication: AWS, Azure, and GCP all offer Data Processing
Agreements. Enabling audit logging on cloud services so that processor-level
activity can be reviewed is a direct requirement flowing from Article 28 audit
rights obligations.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 30 — RECORDS OF PROCESSING ACTIVITIES (RoPA)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (paragraph 1): "Each controller and, where applicable, the controller's
representative, shall maintain a record of processing activities under its
responsibility. That record shall contain all of the following information:
  (a) the name and contact details of the controller and, where applicable,
      the joint controller, the controller's representative and the DPO;
  (b) the purposes of the processing;
  (c) a description of the categories of data subjects and personal data;
  (d) the categories of recipients to whom personal data have been or will be
      disclosed, including recipients in third countries or international
      organisations;
  (e) where applicable, transfers of personal data to a third country or an
      international organisation;
  (f) where possible, the envisaged time limits for erasure of different
      categories of data;
  (g) where possible, a general description of the technical and organisational
      security measures referred to in Article 32(1)."
 
Full text (paragraph 2): Processors must maintain an equivalent record of
processing categories carried out on behalf of each controller, including:
  (a) the name and contact details of the processor and each controller;
  (b) the categories of processing carried out on behalf of each controller;
  (c) where applicable, transfers to third countries;
  (d) where possible, a general description of the security measures.
 
Full text (paragraph 3): These records shall be in writing, including
electronic form.
 
Full text (paragraph 4): The controller or processor and, where applicable,
the controller's or processor's representative, shall make the record available
to the supervisory authority on request.
 
Exceptions (paragraph 5): The obligation does not apply to organizations with
fewer than 250 employees unless the processing is likely to result in a risk to
the rights and freedoms of data subjects, unless the processing is not occasional,
or unless the processing includes special categories (Article 9) or criminal
conviction data (Article 10).
 
Cloud security implication: Cloud-based processing activities (e.g., storing
customer data in S3, processing transactions via RDS, running user authentication
via IAM) must all be documented in the RoPA. The record should describe the cloud
service, the data involved, the retention period, and the security measures in
place. This is a mandatory document that must be producible on supervisory authority
request.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 32(1)(d) — REGULAR TESTING AND EVALUATION OF SECURITY MEASURES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text: "...a process for regularly testing, assessing and evaluating the
effectiveness of technical and organisational measures for ensuring the security
of the processing."
 
This sub-clause of Article 32 is specifically audit-relevant because it requires
organizations to not only implement security measures but to periodically verify
that those measures are working as intended.
 
Audit evidence this generates:
  - Penetration testing reports and remediation records
  - Vulnerability scanning results and patch logs
  - Security review records for cloud configurations
  - Results of regular access reviews and privilege audits
  - Records of security control effectiveness assessments
 
Cloud security implication: Enabling services such as AWS Config, AWS Security
Hub, Azure Security Center, or GCP Security Command Center satisfies the intent
of Article 32(1)(d) by providing ongoing, documented evaluation of cloud security
configurations. Logs and alert histories from these services are audit evidence.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 33(5) — DOCUMENTATION OF PERSONAL DATA BREACHES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text: "The controller shall document any personal data breaches, comprising
the facts relating to the personal data breach, its effects and the remedial
action taken. That documentation shall enable the supervisory authority to verify
compliance with this Article."
 
This is one of the most specific audit evidence requirements in the GDPR. All
personal data breaches must be documented regardless of whether they are notified
to the supervisory authority.
 
The breach register must contain:
  - Date and time of breach discovery
  - Description of the breach (what happened)
  - Categories and approximate number of data subjects affected
  - Categories and approximate number of personal data records affected
  - Description of likely consequences of the breach
  - Description of remedial measures taken or proposed
  - Reason for not notifying the supervisory authority, if notification was
    not made (i.e., why the breach was unlikely to result in risk)
 
Cloud security implication: All cloud-side security incidents affecting personal
data must be logged and documented. Enabling CloudTrail (AWS), Azure Monitor, or
GCP Cloud Audit Logs ensures there is a technical record of unauthorized access,
data exfiltration attempts, or misconfiguration events that may constitute a
breach. These logs feed into the mandatory breach register.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 35 — DATA PROTECTION IMPACT ASSESSMENT (DPIA)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Full text (summary): Where a type of processing is likely to result in a high
risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons, the controller shall carry
out a data protection impact assessment prior to the processing.
 
A DPIA must include:
  1. A systematic description of the envisaged processing operations and their
     purposes
  2. An assessment of the necessity and proportionality of the processing
  3. An assessment of the risks to the rights and freedoms of data subjects
  4. The measures envisaged to address the risks
 
DPIAs are mandatory for:
  - Systematic profiling with legal or similarly significant effects
  - Large-scale processing of special categories of data (Article 9)
  - Large-scale, systematic monitoring of a publicly accessible area
 
DPIAs are strong audit evidence that demonstrates the organization thought
through data protection risks before processing began, rather than retroactively.
 
Cloud security implication: New cloud deployments processing personal data at
scale — particularly those involving customer databases, health records, financial
data, or behavioral analytics — should have a DPIA. The DPIA should document the
cloud services used, the security controls applied, and the residual risks accepted.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 37–39 — DATA PROTECTION OFFICER (DPO)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Organizations required to appoint a DPO must document:
  - The identity and contact details of the DPO
  - Evidence that the DPO was involved in all data protection matters
  - Records of DPO advice and the organization's response to that advice
 
Article 39 tasks the DPO with monitoring compliance. DPO activity logs and
reports are audit evidence that an active compliance monitoring function exists.
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ARTICLE 58 — SUPERVISORY AUTHORITY INVESTIGATIVE POWERS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Supervisory authorities have broad powers to request audit evidence directly,
including:
  - Ordering the controller to provide any information needed for the authority's
    tasks
  - Carrying out investigations in the form of data protection audits
  - Obtaining access to all personal data and information necessary to perform
    its tasks
  - Obtaining access to the controller's or processor's premises and processing
    equipment
 
These powers mean that the documentary record described across Articles 5(2),
24, 28, 30, 32, 33, and 35 must be organized and accessible, not just stored
somewhere. The supervisory authority can and does request these documents during
investigations and routine audits.

CLOUD CONFIGURATION IMPLICATIONS — SUMMARY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
GDPR audit evidence requirements translate directly into cloud configuration
recommendations:
 
1. ENABLE AUDIT LOGGING ON ALL SERVICES
   - AWS: CloudTrail (API audit), S3 access logs, RDS audit logs, Lambda logs
     in CloudWatch, CloudFront access logs
   - Azure: Azure Monitor, Activity Log, Diagnostic Logs for each service
   - GCP: Cloud Audit Logs (Admin Activity, Data Access, System Event)
   - Rationale: Article 30 (RoPA), Article 32(1)(d) (effectiveness testing),
     Article 33(5) (breach documentation)
 
2. RETAIN LOGS FOR A DEFINED PERIOD
   - Log retention period should be documented and justified based on purpose
   - Minimum recommendation for GDPR audit trail: 12 months (operational),
     longer for breach investigation evidence
   - Rationale: Article 30(1)(f) (envisaged time limits), Article 33(5)
 
3. PROTECT LOG INTEGRITY
   - Logs must not be alterable by application-level users
   - Use write-once storage (S3 Object Lock, Azure immutable storage, GCP WORM)
   - Rationale: Article 5(1)(f) (integrity and confidentiality)
 
4. RESTRICT ACCESS TO LOGS
   - Logs containing personal data are themselves personal data under GDPR
   - Access should be limited to authorized personnel
   - MFA should be enforced for access to audit trail systems
   - Rationale: Article 25 (data protection by design), Article 32
 
5. DOCUMENT CLOUD SECURITY CONFIGURATIONS
   - Configuration decisions (e.g., "encryption enabled because Article 32")
     should be recorded
   - Rationale: Article 24, Article 5(2) accountability
 
6. ENABLE VULNERABILITY AND COMPLIANCE MONITORING
   - AWS Config, Azure Policy, GCP Security Command Center provide ongoing
     compliance monitoring records
   - These outputs are audit evidence under Article 32(1)(d)
   - Rationale: Article 32(1)(d) regular testing and evaluation
 
7. MAINTAIN A BREACH REGISTER
   - All cloud security incidents affecting personal data must be logged
   - Even non-notifiable incidents must be documented
   - Rationale: Article 33(5)
 
8. DOCUMENT PROCESSOR RELATIONSHIPS
   - Each cloud provider acting as a data processor must have a DPA in place
   - Evidence of DPA review and execution must be retained
   - Rationale: Article 28
 
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PENALTIES FOR AUDIT EVIDENCE FAILURES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Under Article 83(4), failures to maintain records (Article 30) or cooperate
with the supervisory authority (Article 31) can result in fines of up to:
  - EUR 10,000,000, or
  - 2% of the total worldwide annual turnover of the preceding financial year,
    whichever is higher.
 
Under Article 83(5), more serious violations including failure to implement
appropriate security measures (Article 32) or failure to carry out a DPIA
(Article 35) can result in fines of up to:
  - EUR 20,000,000, or
  - 4% of total worldwide annual turnover,
    whichever is higher.
